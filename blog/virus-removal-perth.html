Signs Your Computer Has a Virus or Malware

Viruses and malware rarely announce themselves. They operate silently in the background, slowing your computer, stealing data, and causing damage while you work or browse. Here are the warning signs Perth customers most commonly bring to us — if you recognise any of these, your computer almost certainly needs a professional clean.

🐢
PC Running Slow for No Reason
Malware consumes CPU and RAM constantly in the background — making even simple tasks like opening a browser feel sluggish.
🪟
Constant Pop-Ups & Ads
Unexpected pop-up windows, especially ads that appear even when your browser is closed, are a classic sign of adware infection.
🔀
Browser Keeps Redirecting
Your homepage has changed, searches go to a different engine, or links open websites you didn't click on — this is browser hijacking.
🌡️
PC Running Unusually Hot
Cryptomining malware forces your CPU and GPU to work at full capacity 24/7, causing extreme heat even when you're doing nothing.
🔒
Files Locked or Encrypted
If you're seeing ransom messages or your files have strange new extensions and won't open, you've been hit by ransomware.
❓
Unfamiliar Programs Appeared
New toolbars, browser extensions, or programs in your taskbar that you didn't install are a strong indicator of malware.
📶
Unexplained High Data Usage
Some malware sends your data or uses your internet connection as part of a botnet. If your data usage has spiked, something is running in the background.
💥
Crashes or Blue Screen Errors
Frequent system crashes, freeze-ups, or blue screens that appeared suddenly — especially after downloading something — can indicate a severe infection.

Stay Safe

Tech Support Scams — What to Watch Out For

Perth residents are frequently targeted by tech support scammers who use fake virus warnings to steal money or access. Knowing the difference between a real infection and a scam can save you hundreds of dollars.

⚠️
These are ALWAYS scams — ignore them
  • A pop-up says your PC is infected and tells you to call a 1300 or 1800 number
  • A webpage locks your screen and claims to be "Microsoft" or "Windows Security"
  • Someone calls you out of the blue claiming to be from Microsoft or Telstra saying your PC has a virus
  • A pop-up asks you to download "security software" to remove a detected threat
  • A warning appears in your browser that will not close, showing a phone number

Legitimate antivirus software and Microsoft never ask you to call a phone number. If you have clicked on one of these, do not call the number, do not download anything, and do not give anyone remote access to your PC. Message us and we will tell you whether any real action is needed.

Stay Protected

How to Avoid Getting Infected Again

The best virus removal job is the one that is the last one you ever need. After every clean we give you honest advice on staying safe — here are the most important habits to build.

Keep Windows Update turned on. The majority of malware exploits known security vulnerabilities in Windows. When Microsoft releases a security patch, attackers immediately target every unpatched machine. Letting updates install automatically closes these holes before criminals can use them against you.

Use a real antivirus, not just Windows Defender. Windows Defender has improved significantly in recent years and is a reasonable baseline. But for better protection — especially against newer malware — a paid solution like Malwarebytes Premium, Bitdefender or ESET provides real-time protection that catches threats Defender misses. We can recommend the right option for your situation without any sales pressure.

Do not download software from random websites. The majority of malware infections we see in Perth came through a "free" download — a cracked game, a free video editor, a tool downloaded from a Google ad instead of the real website. If you are downloading software, go directly to the developer's official website. Be suspicious of any file that comes as a .exe from a site you do not recognise.

Be careful with email attachments. Phishing emails that pretend to be from Australia Post, the ATO, banks, or Telstra are the most common malware delivery method. If an email asks you to download or open an attachment and you were not expecting it, do not open it — even if it looks like it is from someone you know. Call them to verify first.

Use a proper password manager. Credential-stealing malware often targets saved passwords in browsers. Using a dedicated password manager like Bitwarden (free) or 1Password keeps your passwords out of the browser's built-in storage and significantly reduces risk if you do get infected.
