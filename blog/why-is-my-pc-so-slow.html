1. Too many programs starting with Windows

This is the number one reason I see for slow boot times in Perth homes. Every time you install new software — a game, a printer driver, a download manager, Spotify — it often adds itself silently to your startup list. Over months and years this snowballs. Your PC ends up trying to load 20 to 40 background programs before you can even open a browser tab.

The result is a machine that takes three to six minutes to become usable after turning on, even if the hardware itself is perfectly fine.

What to do: Press Ctrl + Shift + Esc to open Task Manager, then click the Startup tab. You will see everything that loads when Windows starts. Right-click and disable anything you do not recognise or do not need immediately at startup. Be careful to leave antivirus software and any hardware drivers enabled.

Perth tip: After doing this one step, most customers report a noticeably faster boot. It costs nothing and takes under five minutes to try.

2. Your storage drive is nearly full

Windows uses a portion of your hard drive or SSD as virtual memory — essentially as an overflow tank for your RAM. When your drive drops below 10 to 15 percent free space, this system breaks down. Windows cannot write temporary files efficiently, programs take longer to open, and your whole system slows to a crawl.

This is especially noticeable on older spinning hard drives (HDDs) where read and write speeds are already limited. A nearly full HDD can make a modern machine feel like it is running in quicksand.

What to do: Open File Explorer, right-click your C: drive and check Properties. If you are below 15 percent free space, clear out your Downloads folder, empty the Recycle Bin, and uninstall programs you no longer use. Windows also has a built-in tool called Storage Sense under Settings that automates this cleanup.

3. You are still running a spinning hard drive

If your PC takes more than 90 seconds to boot, this is almost certainly the cause. Spinning hard drives (HDDs) read and write data at around 80 to 160MB per second. A modern SSD does the same job at 500 to 3500MB per second. That is anywhere from 6 to 40 times faster depending on the drive.

Upgrading from an HDD to an SSD is the single most impactful hardware change you can make to an older PC. Every customer who has had this done through me has been genuinely surprised by the result.

Real example from Thornlie: A customer brought in a 2017 laptop that took 4 minutes and 20 seconds to boot. The laptop had 8GB of RAM and a decent processor — but a spinning hard drive that was 94 percent full. After an SSD upgrade and data transfer, it booted in under 11 seconds. Same laptop. Same Windows installation. Just a different drive.

What to do: An SSD upgrade with full data transfer starts from $65 labour only if you supply the SSD, or from $150 all-in including a Samsung or Crucial 500GB SSD supplied and fitted. It is the best money you will spend on any computer that is more than three years old.

4. Malware or a virus is running in the background

Malicious software constantly uses your CPU, RAM and network connection behind the scenes. Some malware mines cryptocurrency using your hardware. Some sends spam emails. Some sits silently logging your keystrokes. All of it makes your PC slow.

A sudden and unexplained slowdown — particularly if your PC was running fine and then abruptly got worse — is one of the classic signs of a malware infection. Other signs include your browser redirecting to unexpected pages, unfamiliar programs in your taskbar, or unusually high CPU usage when you are not running anything demanding.

What to do: Download and run a free scan with Malwarebytes. The free version is effective for a one-off manual scan. If it finds threats, remove them and restart. If the slowness continues, or if the infection keeps returning, a professional clean is the safer option — some malware actively resists removal and reinstalls itself.

Warning: Do not trust pop-ups telling you that your PC has a virus and you need to call a number. This is a scam called a tech support scam. Legitimate antivirus software never asks you to call a phone number.

5. Not enough RAM for what you are doing

Random Access Memory (RAM) is your PC's short-term working memory. When you run out of it, Windows starts using a section of your hard drive as overflow — a process called paging. If you are on an HDD this is catastrophic for performance. Even on an SSD it causes noticeable lag.

Windows 11 requires 4GB of RAM minimum, but in practice 4GB is barely enough to run the operating system alone. If you have a browser open with five tabs, an email client, and Spotify running alongside anything else, you are likely hitting your RAM ceiling regularly.

What to do: Press Ctrl + Shift + Esc, click Performance, then Memory. Check the percentage in use while doing your normal tasks. If you are consistently above 80 percent, a RAM upgrade will make a real difference. Upgrading to 16GB covers most everyday users comfortably. RAM upgrades start from $55 labour only if you supply the RAM.

6. Overheating is causing the processor to throttle itself

Modern CPUs and GPUs have a built-in self-protection mechanism called thermal throttling. When the chip reaches a dangerously high temperature, it automatically reduces its own speed to prevent permanent damage. This is why some PCs feel fine when you first turn them on but get noticeably slower after 20 to 30 minutes of use.

The most common causes of overheating are dust buildup blocking the airflow through the case, dried-out thermal paste between the CPU and its cooler, and fans that are failing or running too slowly. Desktop PCs accumulate dust over years in ways that genuinely choke airflow, and laptops are even worse because of their compact design and restricted ventilation.

What to do: If you have a desktop, use compressed air to clear dust from the vents, heatsink and fans. Never use a vacuum cleaner — the static electricity it generates can damage components. If cleaning does not help, or if you have a laptop, the thermal paste between the CPU and cooler may need replacing. This dries out and loses effectiveness every three to five years and is one of the most common causes of laptops slowing down over time. A full clean and thermal paste replacement starts from $89.

  • ✓ PC gets slower after extended use — likely thermal throttling
  • ✓ Fans are very loud and spinning at maximum — airflow is restricted
  • ✓ Laptop is very hot to touch on the bottom — thermal paste has dried
  • ✓ System crashes or shuts off unexpectedly — critical overheating

7. Windows itself needs a clean reinstall

Windows accumulates years of digital debris — broken registry entries, leftover files from uninstalled programs, corrupted system files, fragmented data. No amount of optimisation fully fixes a Windows installation that has been running on the same machine for four or five years without ever being rebuilt.

A clean Windows reinstall does not mean losing your files. The process involves backing up all your data first, then wiping and rebuilding Windows from scratch, then returning all your personal files and setting up your software again. Done properly, it can make a five-year-old PC feel genuinely new.

How to diagnose your slow PC quickly

If you are not sure which of these seven causes applies to your machine, here is a simple process for narrowing it down:

  • Boot time over 2 minutes: Almost certainly a startup program overload or an old spinning hard drive. Check startup items first, then check what type of drive you have.
  • Slow after 20–30 minutes of use: Thermal throttling. Check for dust and heat.
  • Sudden slowdown that came out of nowhere: Malware is the first thing to rule out. Run Malwarebytes.
  • Slow when you have multiple programs open: RAM is the bottleneck. Check Task Manager's Performance tab.
  • Everything is slow all the time, even with one program: Likely the drive (HDD vs SSD) or a Windows installation that needs rebuilding.
