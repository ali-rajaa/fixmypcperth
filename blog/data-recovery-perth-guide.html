There is no sound more gut-wrenching than a clicking hard drive. Whether it is a decade of family photos, your business tax records, a university thesis you have spent months writing, or years of irreplaceable memories, losing access to your files feels like a crisis. It can happen without any warning at all. One moment everything is fine, and the next your computer will not boot, your drive is making noises it never made before, or a folder full of important documents has simply vanished.

The most important thing to understand right now is this: in the majority of cases, the data is still physically present on the drive. It is not gone. It is inaccessible. There is a significant difference between the two, and the actions you take in the next few minutes will determine which of those situations you are actually in when a professional eventually looks at it. This guide will walk you through exactly what to do, in what order, and just as importantly, what never to do under any circumstances.

Step 1: Stop What You Are Doing and Assess the Situation

The single most important thing you can do right now, before anything else, is stop using the drive. If your computer is still on and the drive is making unusual sounds, shut it down. If you can hear clicking, grinding, buzzing, or repeated whirring sounds coming from inside the machine or from an external drive, those sounds are the read and write heads inside the drive physically struggling. Every additional second the drive operates in that state risks permanent mechanical damage to the platters where your data is stored.

Hard drive platters spin at somewhere between 5,400 and 7,200 rotations per minute. The read and write heads float just nanometres above the surface. When the mechanism begins to fail, those heads can contact the surface, and each contact removes a layer of the magnetic coating that stores your data. This is not a software problem that can be reversed. Once that coating is gone, the data it held is gone with it. Turning the drive off is not giving up. It is the most protective thing you can do.

Urgent: If your drive is making any clicking, grinding, or buzzing sounds, shut the computer down immediately. Do not attempt to copy files. Do not restart. Every second the drive continues to spin increases the risk of permanent data loss.

Step 2: Identify the Type of Failure

Not all data loss is the same, and understanding which category your situation falls into determines every decision that follows. There are two broad categories of hard drive failure and they require completely different approaches.

A logical failure means the drive is physically healthy and functioning normally, but something has gone wrong with the data or file system that sits on top of the hardware. Examples include accidentally deleting a folder or a partition, accidentally formatting a drive, a file system that has become corrupted, a ransomware attack that has encrypted your files, or a Windows installation that has become so damaged it can no longer read the drive structure. In logical failure cases the drive will typically still spin normally, it will still be detected by the computer or appear in Disk Management, and it will not be making any unusual noises. Logical failures have a very high recovery rate when handled correctly.

A physical failure means something has gone wrong with the hardware of the drive itself. This includes mechanical failures such as clicking or grinding noises from the read and write heads, an electric board failure caused by a power surge, a drive that was dropped or physically damaged, a drive that was exposed to water or extreme heat, or a drive that simply will not spin up at all. Physical failures are significantly more complex to recover from. Some can be resolved with component-level repair. Others require specialist clean room facilities where the drive is opened in a controlled environment free of dust and static. In Perth, we handle Level 1 and Level 2 physical recoveries in the workshop and refer more complex clean room cases to trusted specialist labs in Australia.

  • ✓ Drive spins normally, no unusual sounds, files missing or deleted — likely logical failure
  • ✓ Drive not showing up in Windows at all but no sounds — could be logical or electrical
  • ✓ Clicking, grinding, or buzzing noises — physical mechanical failure, stop immediately
  • ✓ Drive was dropped, exposed to water, or subjected to a power surge — physical failure
  • ✓ Drive spins up for a few seconds then stops — likely electrical or firmware issue

Step 3: What You Must Never Do

This section matters more than any other part of this guide. The majority of permanently unrecoverable data loss situations we see in Perth happen not because of the original failure but because of what was done afterwards. Every item below is something a customer has tried before coming to us, and in each case it made the situation significantly worse.

  • ✕ Do not run chkdsk on a failing drive. This Windows tool is designed to repair file systems on healthy drives. On a mechanically failing drive it will attempt thousands of read and write operations at exactly the moment when the hardware can least tolerate them, and it will frequently destroy the very data you are trying to save.
  • ✕ Do not install recovery software onto the failing drive. If you download a recovery program and save it to the same drive that has lost files, you are actively overwriting the deleted data you are trying to retrieve. Always download software to a completely separate drive and run it from there.
  • ✕ Do not open the drive. Hard drives must only be opened inside a certified clean room environment. A single speck of dust settling on the platter is large enough relative to the read and write head clearance to cause a catastrophic head crash. Opening a drive outside a clean room will almost always destroy it completely.
  • ✕ Do not try the freezer trick. This is old advice that circulates online and it is harmful. Putting a hard drive in the freezer causes condensation to form on the internal components when the drive warms back up. Plugging in a drive with condensation on the circuitry will short the electronics and can permanently destroy a board that could otherwise have been repaired.
  • ✕ Do not keep restarting the computer hoping it will fix itself. Each restart puts the drive through another spin-up cycle. If the drive is physically failing, repeated spin-up attempts accelerate the mechanical wear and reduce the window available for professional recovery.
  • ✕ Do not save any new files to the drive. If you have accidentally deleted files, those files are still present in the space the drive has marked as available for reuse. Every new file you save to that drive has a chance of overwriting exactly what you need to recover. Stop writing to the drive the moment you realise something has gone wrong.
Important: Running chkdsk on a physically failing drive is one of the most common causes of permanent data loss we see. If you are unsure whether your drive is healthy, do not run any repair tools. Bring it in for a free assessment first.

Step 4: What You Can Try Yourself for Logical Failures

If your drive is spinning quietly, making no unusual noises, and the situation is a straightforward accidental deletion or format, there is one approach you can try yourself before calling a professional. The key rule is that any software you use must be installed on a different drive, and any files you recover must be saved to a different drive. Not a different folder on the same drive. A physically separate drive.

For simple file deletions, Recuva by Piriform is free and effective for everyday users. Download it on a separate computer or a healthy drive, run the installer to a USB stick, then plug that USB into the affected machine and run it from there. When it finds recoverable files, recover them to an external hard drive or a second internal drive, never back to the source drive.

For more complex situations such as formatted drives, corrupted partitions, or cases where Recuva cannot find what you are looking for, PhotoRec is a more powerful open source alternative that works across a wider range of file types and damage scenarios. It is less user-friendly but more thorough. TestDisk, from the same developer, is useful for recovering lost partitions and repairing drive structures without overwriting data.

Rule to remember: Always recover files to a different physical drive. Never recover to the same drive you lost the files from, even if there appears to be space available.

How to Tell if Your Drive Is an HDD or an SSD

The recovery process and your options differ significantly depending on whether you have a traditional spinning hard drive or a solid state drive. Spinning hard drives store data on magnetic platters. Solid state drives store data in flash memory chips. This matters because the failure modes, the warning signs, and the recovery techniques are completely different.

Spinning hard drives give audible warnings when they are failing. The clicking and grinding sounds described earlier are mechanical. SSDs fail silently. They do not click. They do not grind. An SSD can go from working perfectly to completely undetectable with no warning at all. This makes SSD data recovery more difficult in some respects because by the time you know something is wrong, the situation may already be advanced.

If you are unsure which type of drive you have, press Win plus E to open File Explorer, right-click your C drive, select Properties, then the Tools tab, and click Optimise. Windows will tell you whether the selected drive is a hard disk drive or a solid state drive in the description column.

External Hard Drive Recovery in Perth

External hard drives are among the most common recovery cases we see. They are portable, they get knocked off desks, they get disconnected at the wrong moment, and they fail without warning after years of reliable service. The same rules apply to external drives as to internal ones. If it is making unusual sounds, stop using it immediately. If files have simply disappeared without any sounds or errors, try software recovery to a separate drive first.

One situation unique to external drives is damage to the USB to SATA bridge board inside the enclosure. In many cases the drive itself is completely healthy but the board that connects it to the USB port has failed. This is often a straightforward repair — removing the drive from its enclosure and connecting it directly to a desktop computer or via a clean adapter will make it immediately accessible again. If you are seeing your external drive not showing up but can feel or hear it spinning normally, this is worth trying before assuming the drive itself has failed.

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule: How to Never Be in This Position Again

Data recovery exists because backups failed or were never set up. The most cost-effective, stress-free approach to data loss is making sure you never need to recover anything in the first place. The industry standard is called the 3-2-1 rule, and once you set it up it runs automatically without you having to think about it again.

Three copies of your data means the original plus two separate backups. Two different types of media means you do not store both backups in the same place or the same way. If both backups are on external drives sitting next to your computer, a house fire destroys all three copies at once. One copy stored off-site means at minimum one backup exists somewhere physically separate from your home or office. Cloud storage is the most practical off-site solution for most people.

For Perth home users, a practical setup is Windows backup running to an external drive at home combined with OneDrive, Google Drive, or iCloud for automatic cloud backup of your documents and photos. For business users with more complex needs, we offer automated cloud backup as part of our managed IT support packages. A setup like this costs a fraction of what a single data recovery attempt costs, and it runs silently in the background without requiring you to remember to do anything.

One thing to know about cloud storage: Services like OneDrive and Google Drive are not the same as a backup. They are sync services. If you accidentally delete a file or a ransomware attack encrypts your files, the deletion or encryption syncs to the cloud within minutes. A proper backup retains previous versions of your files so you can go back to before the problem occurred. Make sure your backup solution includes version history.
