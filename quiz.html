Why slow computers in Perth are almost always a storage problem

The single most common repair at FixMyPC Perth is an SSD upgrade. It is not even close. Roughly 94 percent of computers that come in labelled as "just slow" leave performing dramatically faster after a storage upgrade — no other parts replaced, no Windows reinstall required. The reason comes down to physics. A mechanical hard drive uses spinning magnetic platters and a read/write head that physically moves across them, like a vinyl record player. That mechanism has been roughly the same speed since the early 2000s.

Modern software, browsers, and operating systems have outpaced that speed by an enormous margin. Windows 11 alone performs hundreds of small read/write operations every minute during normal use. A mechanical drive simply cannot keep up. The result is that your processor, RAM, and even your graphics card spend most of their time idle, waiting for data to arrive from the drive. That is why an SSD upgrade feels so dramatic — you are not replacing the weakest part of your system; you are removing the bottleneck that was throttling every other component simultaneously.

After an SSD upgrade, boot times typically drop from two to five minutes to under fifteen seconds. Programs that took thirty seconds to open now open in two. The machine does not feel new because the hardware is new — it feels new because the hardware that was always capable is finally being allowed to run at its real speed.

How to tell if your computer has a virus or malware

Malware has evolved significantly over the last decade. The days of obvious pop-up storms and ransom demands are largely gone — modern infections are designed to be invisible. The most profitable malware today either mines cryptocurrency using your hardware silently in the background, harvests banking credentials as you type them, or rents access to your machine to a botnet for use in distributed attacks. None of these announce themselves.

The most reliable indicators of a background infection are: high CPU or RAM usage in Task Manager when no programs are open; a computer that runs significantly hotter than usual; Windows Defender or your installed antivirus refusing to open or reporting that it has been turned off; your browser homepage changing without permission; and contacts reporting that they have received strange messages or emails from your account. If you are experiencing two or more of these together, the likelihood of an active infection is very high.

Consumer antivirus tools catch the majority of common infections but struggle with infections that have been deliberately crafted to evade specific scanners. Professional malware removal uses multiple scanning tools including offline boot-time scanners, rootkit detectors, and manual inspection of startup entries and scheduled tasks to find things that real-time antivirus cannot see while Windows is running.

What to do when your PC will not turn on

A PC that refuses to power on is alarming, but the most important thing to understand is that your files are almost certainly safe. In the overwhelming majority of cases where a computer will not start, the fault is in the power delivery or boot process — not in the storage device holding your data. The most common causes are a failed power supply unit, a dead CMOS battery preventing the board from initialising, a faulty motherboard, or in laptops, a failed charging circuit or a battery that has swollen and disconnected itself.

Even when the storage drive itself is the cause — a failed SSD preventing Windows from loading, for example — the data on that drive is almost always recoverable by removing it and reading it in a different machine. The key is not to attempt DIY repairs that might cause secondary damage. Bringing the machine in for a component-level diagnostic is the fastest and safest path to knowing exactly what has failed and what the realistic recovery options are.

Why overheating is more common in Perth than most other cities

Perth summers are among the hottest in any major Australian city, and indoor temperatures without air conditioning regularly exceed 35 degrees Celsius. PC cooling systems are designed around ambient temperatures, and a machine that runs adequately in a 20-degree environment will often begin thermal throttling in a 35-degree room even with clean fans. This is not a fault — it is the machine protecting itself. But it does mean that dust buildup and degraded thermal paste cause noticeable performance problems in Perth homes at a rate higher than in cooler climates.

Thermal paste between the CPU and its cooler is a heat-conducting compound that transfers heat away from the processor. It degrades over three to five years, becoming dry and less effective. Combined with dust that has built up in the heatsink fins and case vents, this can push a CPU to its thermal limit at moderate loads. The fix — a full internal clean and CPU repaste — is straightforward and typically drops idle temperatures by 15 to 20 degrees and load temperatures by 20 to 30 degrees, permanently resolving throttling and fan noise.

Tech explained simply

Why is my computer so slow? A practical guide

Four causes account for the overwhelming majority of slow computers in Perth. Here is how each one works and what fixes it.

Ageing Hard Drive

Old mechanical drives degrade continuously. Even when healthy they are five to ten times slower than a modern SSD. An upgrade fundamentally changes how the machine feels — and it costs less than most people expect, with all data transferred across safely.

Background Malware

Many infections run silently, consuming CPU and RAM for crypto mining or data harvesting. A slow and hot PC with no obvious cause is a strong indicator of an active background infection that standard antivirus has already missed.

Windows Corruption

Years of software updates and registry changes leave broken entries and orphaned files that accumulate invisibly. A clean Windows reinstall removes all of it — and your files are backed up first, so nothing personal is lost in the process.

Insufficient RAM

Modern browsers alone can consume 4 to 6 GB of RAM. If your machine has 4 GB or 8 GB it is likely struggling to keep pace with today's applications. A RAM upgrade allows genuine multitasking again without constant slowdowns.

Virus and malware

How to tell if your computer has a virus

These are the four most reliable indicators that your PC has an active infection rather than a hardware or software issue.

Hijacked Browser

Homepages changing, random search redirects and new toolbars appearing without permission are classic browser malware indicators. These threats often survive reinstalling the browser and require professional removal to fully clear.

High Idle CPU Usage

If Task Manager shows high CPU or RAM usage when nothing is open, malware is very likely running in the background. Crypto-mining infections are the most common cause of this specific pattern in Perth machines today.

Disabled Security Software

Sophisticated infections disable Windows Defender or installed antivirus as their first action. If your security software will not open or stays turned off after being enabled, the system is almost certainly compromised.

Messages Sent Without You

If contacts report receiving strange emails or messages from your account that you did not send, your credentials have been harvested. Change passwords immediately and get the machine scanned before using any banking sites.
